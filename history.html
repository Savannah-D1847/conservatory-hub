A Brief History of Marine Conservation

In 1972, U.S. Congress passed a monumental set of laws which established much of what we call marine conservation today.

These laws include the Coastal Zone Management Act, the Clean Water Act, the Marine Sanctuaries Act, and the Mammal Protection Act. Together, these laws have served to protect oceans and coasts throughout the states.

The United Nations has also developed a treaty called Convention on Biological Diversity.

This treaty is an agreement of wildlife conservation across world oceans. This treaty serves to protect ocean life as well as sustainably develop natural resources.

These laws and treaties are only a small part of the worldwide legislation to protect marine habitats around the world. While over 70% of the earth is covered in water, very little of it is protected from harm.

Conservation groups have established that we must ..."protect at least 30% of the ocean to safeguard marine biodiversity and build ocean resilience."1

Important facts about marine conservation:

  • Every year, over 17 billion pounds of plastic is dumped into the oceans.2
  • Large amounts of plastic waste can cause all of the oxygen in the water to be used up-creating a dead zone.2
  • It is important to keep the ocean healthy as it can help mitigate global warming.2
  • It has been predicted that by 2055, 90% of coral reefs around the world will have been damaged from bleaching.3
  • From 1970-2012, there has been almost a 50% population decline in marine groups around the world.4
